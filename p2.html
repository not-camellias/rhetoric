Bodies INCorporated: There is no True Escape from Reality

Back to Rhetorical Analysis

      Victoria Vesna’s 1996 project Bodies INCorporated is an interactive project through three websites run by a fictional corporation that goes by the same name of the project, in which people could create virtual avatars and interact with other users. While the project on the surface is one of self-expression for the users, the experiences run by the websites reveal the psychological and emotional connections people make with their avatars and the other users within a corporate run virtual reality.
      The main outlet for the project is the three websites, which makes the project interactive for the users and relies on their input for the project to function. The user is first required to create a digital avatar from pre-made digital assets. These assets, or body parts are made from a Virtual Reality Modeling Language (VRML) (Net Art Anthology) and consist of parts from “various genders, sizes, and ages” (Net Art Anthology), along with adding a variety of textures such as “lava” and “blue plastic” (Net Art Anthology) to create a unique avatar. While this gives the user creative freedom in creating their avatar, the user cannot access such without signing a virtual contract. The contract is presented by Bodies INCorporated and the user essentially gives up all of the rights to their own avatar. The creative freedom shown through the customization of a personal avatar is directly juxtaposed with the contract that gives up all rights to such avatar that the user creates. This creates the illusion that the user has full autonomy over themselves in the virtual world, while in reality, the corporation is always watching over the users even if the corporation is not directly controlling the users themselves. Additionally, the three virtual worlds or websites that the avatars/users can exist and interact with are limited within their functions for the users which reiterates the control that the corporation has over the users/avatars. However, the users do not fully realize this as they have the freedom to move between the three websites freely. The whole dynamic showcases how a corporation can have control over those who use it, even without the users full knowing or acknowledging such control that is being imposed on them.
      The first of the three virtual worlds or websites is known as “LIMBO INCorporated”, or rather just as “Limbo”. The appearance of Limbo is that of “a gray, frustrating, rather static and non-descript zone” (Vesna, ACM Siggraph Art Show Archives). Within Limbo, users can access the information of avatars that have been neglected or abandoned by their owners, which is displayed in gray cubes that float around the virtual world space. Given the gray and stark environment Limbo, this virtual world is serves as a data bank for the avatars registered to “Bodies INCorporated”. This mimics not only the look, but also the feel of the corporate world, which is mainly depicted as lifeless or monotonous. What stands out about Limbo functioning as a data bank is the fact that all of the information is not only accessible to the corporation, but rather to the users as well. This reiterates how each user essentially has no real autonomy over their own avatars, leaving all of their data in the hands of the corporation. Furthermore, the fact that other users can access the data left behind from the avatars seemingly distracts from the fact that corporation is in control as being able to access such data gives the user a sense of freedom. Additionally, since a virtual avatar is a personal self-reflection of their user, other users are able to gain an idea of who the user behind the avatar is, and a sense of community is created amongst the users. With the sense of community comes emotional connections that are forged between the users, and so the users begin to operate with each other as peers, completely forgoing the corporation, as these emotional connections also give freedom to the users without realizing the control of the corporation.
      The next of the three virtual worlds is called “NECROPOLIS INCoprorated” or rather just “Necropolis”. The environment is that of a graveyard, with a “a richly textured, baroque atmosphere” (Digital Art Archive), which is a stark contrast to Limbo, as this place is meant to be more stimulating and lively, despite it being a graveyard. To the users it is said to incur “feelings of anxiety, fear, and exhilaration” (Vesna, ACM Siggraph Art Show Archives) within the users. The purpose of Necropolis is to show all of the possibilities in which a user may choose to “kill” off their avatar, with the graves belonging to the “dead” avatars. While morbid, the fact that the user can choose the death for avatar is another avenue of freedom that is given to the users by the corporation. However, this freedom in death is not without its costs. When a user chooses to “kill” their avatar, it is announced to all of the other users through email. This turns the death into a spectacle for all to see, as the user and avatar are identified and all of the other users can turn and cast judgement on the “dead” user/avatar. Granted, the way that the virtual “deaths” are handled are a stark opposite to how death is treated in the real world. In reality, the societal standard is that death is treated with humility and privacy. The corporation turns this notion on its head, and in turn the response of the users to an avatar’s death is also in stark contrast with the societal standards. Furthermore, the main reason that death is turned into a spectacle in the virtual world comes back to the fact that the user signed away the rights to their avatar, and now the corporation can use the deaths of these avatars to shift and manipulate the dynamics of the virtual community. In effect, the users will turn to each other as they cast their judgement, whether for better or worse on the dead, thus not only showcasing how the users can create complex group dynamics within a virtual setting and also how the power and control of the corporation can be overlooked as the community focuses on their peers.
     The third and last of the virtual worlds or websites is known as “SHOWPLACE!!! INCorporated” or rather as “Exhibition”. This website functions as the main point of interaction between the users in which they can “participate in discussion forums, view star/featured bodies of the week, bet in the deadpools, and enter "dead" or "alive" chat sessions” (Digital Art Archive). The main feature of the virtual world is that any one user can freely alter the avatar of another user without any notice or permission. As the main point of user interaction, this where the virtual community flourishes the most. The users establish connections through the chat rooms and the betting pools, and even a hierarchy can be established through the featured gallery as these are the places where social interactions are simulated, creating real emotions within the users. While there is freedom of community, Exhibition also creates a freedom through the control of others. As anyone can alter another user’s avatar within Exhibition, the user has no bodily autonomy, which can lead to more complex dynamics beyond comradery within the virtual community as users want to represent themselves through their avatars, not become an expression of someone else, especially since an avatar is the user’s main form of control. This is once again a result of the user signing away the rights of their avatar, however, instead of the corporation having control, it is the other users that have the control. Since the users are the ones with the control, they undermine the fact that it is really the corporation that gives them control. Due to this, the users fall right into the system, creating their own community and connections, ones intwined with comradery and judgement for each other all without realizing that the corporation looms above them all.
      Although the project Bodies INCorporated was mainly an online installment, it manages to explore ideas of virtual self-expression, online group dynamics, and what it means to exist under capitalistic control. The project comes at a time when the new age of the internet and information technology are still coming into fruition, and are still years out from their rapid evolution in the 21st century. The project served not only as a reflection on the future of selfexpression, but also how people exist under the control of corporations, even if they do not fully realize it. While Bodies INCorporated is not intended to be a call to action against capitalism, it fully showcases how people are under some control, even when they have a myriad of freedoms right at their hands. In contrast, the project also explores how people shift and change as the standards are different in the virtual world compared to the real world, and how people respond to such changes, usually for the worse. However, even as the online world continues to grow and change, the ideals of expression, community, and control evolve as well.
